Every Song Has a Digital Identity

Every professionally released song has something fans never see.

A Digital Identity™.

Just as every person has a passport or driver's licence, every recording carries information that tells the global music industry exactly what it is—and who should be paid.

This information is called metadata.

Metadata helps identify:

  • Artist
  • Song Title
  • Songwriters
  • Publishers
  • Record Label
  • ISRC
  • UPC
  • ISWC
  • Ownership Splits
  • Copyright Information
  • Performing Rights Organization (PRO)
  • Distribution Information

Without accurate metadata, your music becomes much harder for royalty systems to recognize and process correctly.

What Is an ISRC?

One of the most important parts of your recording's Digital Identity™ is the International Standard Recording Code (ISRC).

Think of an ISRC as the permanent identification number for a specific recording.

For example:

  • Original Version → One ISRC
  • Acoustic Version → Different ISRC
  • Live Version → Different ISRC
  • Remix → Different ISRC
  • Dolby Atmos Mix → Different ISRC

Even though they're all the same song, they're different recordings.

The ISRC helps streaming platforms, distributors, broadcasters, and royalty organizations recognize exactly which recording is being played.

Small Mistakes Can Create Big Problems

Metadata errors are rarely dramatic.

Most begin as simple mistakes.

  • A misspelled artist name.
  • An incorrect songwriter credit.
  • Missing publisher information.
  • An outdated ownership split.
  • A duplicated ISRC.
  • Incorrect recording information.

One mistake may seem insignificant.

But when your music is traveling across dozens of platforms and multiple countries, those small inconsistencies can make matching and processing information far more difficult.

Where Metadata Problems Begin

Metadata issues often happen during everyday business activities, including:

  • Releasing music too quickly.
  • Switching distributors.
  • Signing with a new publisher.
  • Uploading remixes or alternate versions.
  • Importing older catalogs.
  • Manual data entry errors.
  • Different collaborators submitting different information.

As your catalog grows, so does the importance of keeping every piece of information accurate and consistent.
