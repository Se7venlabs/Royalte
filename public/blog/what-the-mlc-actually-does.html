If You Don't Know What The MLC Is, You're Not Alone

Ask ten independent artists what The MLC does, and chances are most won't have a clear answer.

Some believe it's a Performing Rights Organization.

Others think it's another music distributor.

Some have never heard of it at all.

The truth is, The Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC) plays an important role in today's music industry—particularly for songwriters, composers, and music publishers in the United States.

If you're writing original music and it's being streamed on digital services, understanding what The MLC does can help you better understand where your royalties come from and why proper registration matters.

What Is The MLC?

The Mechanical Licensing Collective, commonly known as The MLC, is a nonprofit organization established under the U.S. Music Modernization Act (MMA).

Its primary responsibility is to administer blanket mechanical licenses for eligible digital music services in the United States.

In simple terms, The MLC helps collect and distribute certain mechanical royalties generated from interactive streaming and digital downloads.

These royalties are distinct from performance royalties and recording royalties.

What Are Mechanical Royalties?

Every song actually consists of two separate copyrights.

1. The Sound Recording

This is the recorded performance.

Typically owned by:

  • Recording artists
  • Record labels
  • Independent musicians

2. The Musical Composition

This is the underlying song itself.

It includes:

  • Lyrics
  • Melody
  • Harmony
  • Song structure

Mechanical royalties are paid for the use of this musical composition.

If you wrote the song, you may be entitled to these royalties.

Where Do Mechanical Royalties Come From?

When listeners stream music on participating digital music services, different royalty streams are created.

Depending on your role, you may receive:

  • Recording royalties
  • Performance royalties
  • Mechanical royalties

The MLC focuses specifically on eligible digital mechanical royalties within the United States.

Understanding the distinction between these royalty types helps artists build a clearer picture of how music income flows.

Who Should Register With The MLC?

If you are:

  • A songwriter
  • A composer
  • A self-published artist
  • A music publisher
  • A publishing administrator

...it's worth understanding whether registering with The MLC is appropriate for your publishing interests.

Registration helps The MLC associate eligible royalty payments with the correct rights holders.

What Happens If You Don't Register?

Royalties don't automatically know where to go.

The music industry relies on accurate rights information to connect royalty payments with the correct owners.

If ownership information is incomplete or unavailable, distributing royalties accurately becomes much more difficult.

That's why maintaining accurate publishing information is so important.

Common Misconceptions About The MLC

"The MLC collects all of my royalties."

It doesn't.

The MLC administers a specific category of mechanical royalties.

Artists may also receive royalties through performing rights organizations, distributors, neighboring rights organizations, labels, and other collection entities depending on their rights and agreements.

"My distributor handles everything."

Music distributors typically deliver recordings to streaming platforms.

That doesn't necessarily mean they administer every royalty stream associated with your music.

Understanding what each organization does helps reduce confusion.

"I'm too small to worry about publishing."

Publishing matters regardless of audience size.

Even artists just beginning their careers benefit from organizing their publishing information correctly.

Good habits established early can save significant time later.

Why Metadata Matters Here Too

The MLC relies on accurate rights information.

That includes metadata such as:

  • Song titles
  • Songwriter names
  • Ownership information
  • Publishing details

Accurate metadata helps connect songs with the correct rights holders.

As your catalog grows, maintaining this information becomes increasingly important.
