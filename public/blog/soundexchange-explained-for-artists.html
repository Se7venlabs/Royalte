Why Does SoundExchange Exist?

Music generates many different kinds of royalties.

Rather than one organization collecting every payment, the industry relies on specialized organizations that focus on different rights and different uses of music.

SoundExchange exists to administer a specific category of royalties related to certain digital performances of sound recordings.

Its role is specialized.

It isn't a distributor.

It isn't a record label.

It isn't a Performing Rights Organization (PRO).

Understanding where SoundExchange fits helps artists build a clearer picture of how today's royalty ecosystem works.

What Is SoundExchange?

SoundExchange is a U.S.-based nonprofit organization that administers and distributes certain digital performance royalties generated from eligible non-interactive digital transmissions of sound recordings.

Examples may include certain forms of:

  • Internet radio
  • Satellite radio
  • Other eligible digital audio services

Unlike a PRO, which focuses on performance royalties for musical compositions, SoundExchange focuses on a different copyright and a different royalty stream.

That's why artists often confuse the two.

Every Song Contains Two Separate Copyrights

One of the biggest reasons music royalties seem complicated is because every commercially released song actually contains two distinct copyrights.

Understanding this concept makes organizations like SoundExchange much easier to understand.

Copyright #1 — The Sound Recording

This is the recorded performance people hear.

It is commonly owned or controlled by:

  • Recording artists
  • Independent musicians
  • Record labels

This is often referred to as the master recording.

Copyright #2 — The Musical Composition

This is the song itself.

It includes:

  • Lyrics
  • Melody
  • Harmony
  • Musical arrangement

Songwriters and publishers typically have interests in this copyright.

Because these copyrights are different, different organizations administer different royalty streams.

Who Typically Benefits from SoundExchange?

Depending on the recording, the applicable rights, and eligibility requirements, SoundExchange distributions may involve:

  • Featured recording artists
  • Sound recording copyright owners
  • Eligible background musicians and vocalists in certain circumstances

Understanding who participates helps artists better understand why different rights holders may receive different royalty payments.

What SoundExchange Does Not Do

One of the biggest misconceptions in the music industry is believing that one organization handles everything.

SoundExchange has a very specific responsibility.

It does not:

❌ Replace your music distributor

❌ Replace your Performing Rights Organization (PRO)

❌ Replace The MLC

❌ Replace your publisher

❌ Collect every royalty generated by your music

Knowing what SoundExchange doesn't do is just as important as understanding what it does.

Common Misconceptions

"My distributor collects all of my royalties."

Distributors primarily deliver recordings to digital platforms and may collect certain royalty streams depending on your agreement.

That doesn't mean every royalty connected to your music flows through your distributor.

"I already joined a PRO."

A PRO and SoundExchange perform different functions.

Joining one does not replace the role of the other.

Each organization administers different rights.

"SoundExchange pays every artist."

Eligibility depends on the rights involved, the type of digital use, and other factors.

Understanding SoundExchange's role helps artists ask informed questions about their own royalty setup instead of assuming every recording generates the same payments.

Why Metadata Matters Here Too

Behind every royalty payment is data.

Metadata helps connect recordings with the correct rights holders.

Important information includes:

  • Artist name
  • Featured artist
  • Recording title
  • ISRC
  • Sound recording ownership
  • Rights holder information
  • Recording metadata

When this information is accurate and consistent, royalty administration becomes far more effective.

That's one reason metadata remains one of the most valuable assets attached to your music.
