The Biggest Myth in Music Streaming

Most artists believe streaming pays almost nothing.

The reality is a little more complicated.

Streaming services like Spotify, Apple Music, Amazon Music, YouTube Music, and others do pay royalties.

The bigger question is:

Are you collecting everything your music earns?

Because simply uploading your music through a distributor doesn't necessarily mean every available royalty has a path back to you.

That's where registration becomes incredibly important.

One Stream Can Generate Multiple Royalties

When someone presses play, more happens behind the scenes than most artists realize.

A single stream may involve multiple rights—including the sound recording, the musical composition, public performance rights, mechanical rights, publishing income, neighboring rights, and international collections.

These royalties are often collected by different organizations.

That's why one stream has the potential to generate multiple revenue opportunities.

The challenge isn't whether streaming pays.

The challenge is making sure your music is properly represented across the rights ecosystem.

The Registration Gap

Imagine two artists.

Both release the exact same song.

Both receive one million streams.

Artist A uploads their music through a distributor.

Artist B uploads their music and properly registers their rights with the organizations responsible for collecting different types of royalties.

Who has the better opportunity to collect more of the royalties their music generates?

In many cases...

Artist B.

Not because the song performed better.

Not because the streaming platform paid more.

But because more royalty pathways were connected back to the artist.

That's the registration gap.

The Biggest Mistake Artists Make

The biggest mistake artists make isn't releasing music.

It's assuming their distributor collects everything.

Distributors play an important role in getting music onto streaming platforms.

But depending on your situation, there may be additional registrations required for publishing, performance royalties, mechanical royalties, neighboring rights, and international collections.

Understanding what your distributor does—and doesn't do—is one of the smartest business decisions an artist can make.

Streaming Is Global

Your music doesn't stop at your country's borders.

Someone streams your song in Canada.

Another fan discovers it in Germany.

A playlist adds it in Brazil.

A creator uses it in Australia.

Every territory has its own rights organizations, collection systems, and royalty processes.

The more complete your registrations are, the better positioned your music is to participate in those systems.

Why Artists Miss Money

Most artists don't intentionally leave money behind.

They simply don't know how many organizations exist—or what each one does.

Common reasons include:

  • Publishing rights not registered.
  • Missing Performing Rights Organization membership.
  • Mechanical royalties not being collected.
  • Neighboring rights never registered.
  • Metadata inconsistencies.
  • International registrations overlooked.
  • Older releases never updated.
  • Assuming the distributor handles everything.

These aren't unusual mistakes.

They're simply gaps that become more expensive as a catalog grows.

Your Music Business Grows With Your Career

When you release your first song, registration feels simple.

Five years later...

You may have multiple albums.

Collaborations.

Acoustic versions.

Remixes.

International listeners.

Publishing agreements.

New distributors.

The larger your catalog becomes, the harder it is to manually keep track of every registration and every rights organization.

That's why labels, publishers, and experienced rights administrators regularly review their catalogs.

Registration Isn't One-Time

Many artists think registration happens once.

In reality, it's ongoing.

Your catalog changes.

Ownership changes.

Publishing changes.

Metadata changes.

New markets emerge.

Older releases deserve just as much attention as new ones.

Keeping your registrations current helps ensure your music continues working for you long after release day.
