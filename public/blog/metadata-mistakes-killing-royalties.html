The 7 Metadata Mistakes That Could Be Costing You Music Royalties

2026-07-23 · 7 min read

Why the information attached to your music may be just as valuable as the music itself.

Imagine spending months writing the perfect song.

You refine every lyric. You record dozens of vocal takes. You obsess over the mix. The master finally sounds exactly the way you imagined.

Release day arrives. Your song goes live on streaming platforms around the world. Friends begin sharing it. Fans start adding it to playlists. Streams slowly begin to climb.

Months later, your royalty statement arrives. The numbers don't make sense. You assume streaming simply doesn't pay enough.

But what if that wasn't the real problem? What if some of the money couldn't find you because the information attached to your music couldn't find you?

That happens far more often than most artists realize. The music industry runs on data. Every stream, every royalty payment, every playlist placement, every recommendation, and every rights claim begins with one thing: Metadata.

Most artists think they're in the music business. In reality, they're also in the information business. The quality of your metadata can directly influence how your music is identified, discovered, tracked, and ultimately paid.

What Is Music Metadata?

Think of metadata as the shipping label attached to a package.

Your song is the package. Your metadata is the address.

If the address is incomplete, the package may arrive late. It may arrive at the wrong destination. Or it may never arrive at all.

Music works the same way. Metadata tells the industry:

  • Who recorded the song.
  • Who wrote it.
  • Who owns it.
  • Who published it.
  • Which recording is the official version.
  • Where royalties should be sent.
  • How streaming services should organize your catalog.

Without accurate metadata, even an incredible song can become difficult for the music business to understand.

Why Metadata Matters More Than Ever

Artificial intelligence has dramatically increased the speed at which music can be created.

Millions of new tracks are released every year. As the volume of music continues to grow, platforms increasingly rely on structured information—not human judgment—to organize recordings.

That means metadata isn't becoming less important. It's becoming one of the most valuable assets attached to every release.

Accurate metadata improves:

  • Search results
  • Playlist eligibility
  • Catalog organization
  • Royalty reporting
  • Rights management
  • Global distribution
  • Long-term discoverability

AI is only as intelligent as the information it receives.

Poor metadata creates poor intelligence. Great metadata creates better outcomes.

Mistake #1 — Inconsistent Artist Names

One of the simplest mistakes can create some of the biggest headaches.

Consider these examples:

  • Black Alternative
  • BlackAlternative
  • Black Alternative Official
  • Black Alternative Music

To a person, those may all describe the same artist. To digital platforms, they can appear to be entirely different identities.

The result may include:

  • Duplicate artist profiles
  • Split streaming history
  • Confused recommendation systems
  • Lost followers
  • Catalog fragmentation

Your artist name should remain consistent everywhere it appears. Consistency builds a stronger digital identity.

Mistake #2 — Mismanaging Your ISRC Codes

Every sound recording receives an International Standard Recording Code (ISRC).

Think of an ISRC as your recording's permanent fingerprint. Whenever that recording is streamed, sold, licensed, or reported, the ISRC helps identify exactly which master generated the activity.

Problems often occur when artists:

  • Upload an existing master through a new distributor and accidentally assign a new ISRC.
  • Lose track of previously assigned codes.
  • Confuse different versions of the same song.
  • Create duplicate recordings without realizing it.

For example:

A live version. An acoustic version. A Dolby Atmos mix. A remastered release.

Each recording requires careful identification.

Managing ISRCs properly helps preserve the integrity of your catalog over time.

Mistake #3 — Incomplete Songwriter Information

Many artists focus heavily on recordings while overlooking compositions. They're not the same thing.

Every song should clearly identify:

  • Songwriters
  • Composers
  • Lyricists
  • Ownership percentages

Missing or disputed songwriter information can delay publishing royalties until ownership is resolved.

A simple split sheet completed before release can prevent years of confusion later.

Mistake #4 — Missing Publisher Information

Even artists without traditional publishing deals still have publishing metadata.

Publishing information determines how many organizations identify who should receive publishing royalties.

Missing information can complicate:

  • Mechanical royalties
  • Performance royalties
  • International collections
  • Licensing opportunities

Publishing metadata isn't only for major labels. Independent artists benefit just as much from keeping it accurate.

Mistake #5 — Small Release Errors That Create Big Problems

Metadata goes far beyond names and codes. Small details matter.

Examples include:

  • Incorrect release dates
  • Album title inconsistencies
  • Missing featured artists
  • Wrong version names
  • Inconsistent capitalization

While these may appear minor, they affect how platforms organize your catalog and how fans discover your music.

Small mistakes can follow a release for years.

Mistake #6 — Forgetting Global Metadata

Music has become a global business. Your metadata should be prepared for international distribution.

Ask yourself:

  • Is my music available everywhere you intended?
  • Are rights represented correctly across territories?
  • Are alternate versions clearly distinguished?
  • Does every release contain complete international metadata?

Global availability isn't just about distribution. It's about ensuring accurate information flows across countless rights systems around the world.

Mistake #7 — Never Auditing Your Catalog

Many artists assume metadata is entered once and forgotten forever. That's rarely true.

Your catalog evolves. Platforms merge artist profiles. Distributors update systems. Publishing changes. Rights ownership changes. New versions are released.

Without regular audits, inconsistencies accumulate over time. Routine catalog reviews can uncover:

  • Duplicate releases
  • Broken artist links
  • Missing identifiers
  • Incorrect credits
  • Catalog conflicts
  • Metadata inconsistencies

Treat metadata maintenance the same way you treat instrument maintenance. Regular care prevents larger problems later.

Metadata Nobody Talks About

When most artists hear "metadata," they think of an artist name and song title.

In reality, modern music contains dozens of important metadata fields. These may include:

  • Artist Name
  • Song Title
  • Album Title
  • ISRC
  • UPC
  • ISWC
  • Songwriter Credits
  • Composer Credits
  • Producer Credits
  • Record Label
  • Copyright Owner
  • Publisher
  • Featured Artists
  • Release Date
  • Genre
  • Language
  • Explicit Flag
  • Territory Availability

Every one of these fields contributes to how your music is identified throughout the industry.
