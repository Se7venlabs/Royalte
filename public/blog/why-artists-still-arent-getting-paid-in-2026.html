The Biggest Myth in the Music Industry

Many artists believe that once their music is uploaded through a distributor, every royalty automatically finds its way back to them.

Unfortunately, that's simply not how today's music industry works.

A distributor plays an important role in getting your music onto streaming platforms, but distribution is only one piece of a much larger royalty ecosystem.

Every stream, radio play, live performance, television placement, or international use of your music can generate different types of royalties—often collected by different organizations operating independently of one another.

If even one part of that system is incomplete, inaccurate, or disconnected, royalty payments can be delayed, misdirected, or remain unclaimed.

The Music Industry Has Never Been More Complex

Today's artists have more opportunities than ever before.

You no longer need a major record label to release music worldwide.

Within minutes, your music can be available in more than 170 countries.

But that global reach comes with new responsibilities.

Modern music rights are spread across a complex network of organizations, platforms, publishers, distributors, collection societies, and databases.

For many artists, simply understanding who does what has become one of the biggest challenges of running a successful music career.

Ask Yourself These Questions

Before assuming you're collecting every dollar your music has earned, ask yourself:

  • Do you know every organization collecting royalties on your behalf?
  • Do you know who administers your publishing rights?
  • Have all of your songs been properly registered?
  • Are your songwriter splits documented correctly?
  • Is your metadata consistent across every platform?
  • Have you registered for every royalty stream you're eligible to receive?
  • Do you know whether you're collecting international royalties?

If you're unsure about even one of these questions, there's a good chance your music business deserves a closer look.

Music Doesn't Generate Just One Royalty

This surprises many artists.

A single song can generate multiple royalty streams depending on where and how it's used.

These may include:

  • Performance royalties
  • Mechanical royalties
  • Publishing royalties
  • Master recording royalties
  • Digital performance royalties
  • Neighboring rights royalties
  • Synchronization licensing income
  • International royalties
  • Broadcast royalties
  • Public performance royalties

Each royalty type may be collected by different organizations, using different systems, on different schedules.

No single organization collects every royalty generated by your music.

Metadata Is the DNA of Your Music

Behind every professionally released song is an enormous amount of information.

This information tells the global music industry who created the song, who owns it, and who should be paid.

Your metadata may include:

  • Artist name
  • Song title
  • Album information
  • Songwriters
  • Publishers
  • Songwriting ownership percentages
  • ISRC
  • ISWC
  • UPC
  • Recording ownership
  • Publishing ownership
  • Performing Rights Organization affiliations
  • Distribution information

Think of metadata as your music's digital fingerprint.

When that fingerprint is incomplete or inconsistent, royalty systems may struggle to correctly identify who should receive payment.

That's why labels, publishers, distributors, and rights administrators place so much importance on accurate metadata.

The Global Nature of Music Creates New Challenges

Music has no borders.

A song released in Toronto today can be streamed tomorrow in London, licensed next month in Germany, played on radio in Australia, used in a television program in Japan, and performed publicly in Brazil.

Each country may have different collection societies, reporting requirements, and payment systems.

Without accurate registrations and rights management, international royalties can become some of the hardest income to identify and collect.

The Real Problem Isn't Low Streaming Payouts

One of the biggest conversations in music is how little streaming services pay artists.

While payout rates are certainly important, they often distract from another critical issue.

Many artists aren't collecting every royalty they're already entitled to.

In other words...

The problem isn't always that streaming pays too little.

Sometimes the problem is that artists aren't connected to every royalty stream their music is generating.

Understanding that difference can completely change how you manage your music career.

Technology Can Help—But Only If the Data Is Right

Artificial intelligence and music technology are transforming the music business.

But technology is only as good as the information it receives.

If the underlying data is inaccurate, incomplete, or outdated, even the most advanced systems cannot reliably identify every opportunity.

Good technology begins with good data.
