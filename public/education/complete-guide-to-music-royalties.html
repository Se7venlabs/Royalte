Introduction

For decades, independent artists have been told the same thing: write great music and everything else will take care of itself. Unfortunately, that's rarely how today's music industry works.

Every year, millions of songs are released onto streaming platforms around the world. Some generate millions of streams. Some receive radio airplay. Some are licensed for film, television, and advertising. Some quietly build audiences in countries the artist has never visited. Yet despite all these opportunities, countless musicians still struggle to answer one surprisingly simple question:

"How do music royalties actually work?"

It's not because artists lack talent. It's because the modern music business has become incredibly complex. Today's music industry is built on an interconnected network of copyrights, metadata, publishing, distributors, collection societies, neighbouring rights organizations, digital platforms, recommendation engines, and increasingly, artificial intelligence. Every one of these systems depends on accurate information, and every one of them affects how your music is identified, managed, and supported throughout its lifecycle. Understanding that ecosystem is no longer optional — it's one of the most valuable investments an independent artist can make.

Music Is No Longer Just Music

Years ago, releasing music was relatively straightforward. An artist recorded a song. A record label manufactured physical copies. Retail stores sold albums. Radio stations introduced listeners to new music.

Today, your music exists in an entirely different environment. A single release may appear simultaneously on Spotify, Apple Music, Amazon Music, YouTube Music, Deezer, TIDAL, TikTok, Instagram, Facebook, and hundreds of additional digital services. Behind every release is an enormous amount of information that allows these platforms to understand exactly what your music is, who created it, and how it fits into the global music ecosystem. Without that information, even great music can become difficult to identify, organize, or discover.

Why This Guide Exists

The purpose of this guide is simple: to explain the modern music business in plain English. Not legal language. Not industry jargon. Not confusing terminology.

Whether you're releasing your first single or managing a catalog of hundreds of recordings, this guide will help you understand the systems working behind the scenes every time someone presses Play. Along the way, you'll learn about music copyright, publishing, mechanical royalties, performance royalties, neighbouring rights, metadata, Digital Identity™, streaming platforms, artificial intelligence, catalog management, international royalties, rights organizations, and long-term career building.

Most importantly, you'll learn how all of these pieces connect — because they don't exist independently. They form one ecosystem.

The Royaltē Philosophy

One of the biggest misconceptions in the music industry is that royalties are simply about collecting money. They're not. Royalties are the result of an entire ecosystem working correctly — one that depends on accurate ownership, complete registrations, reliable metadata, publishing information, rights administration, digital identity, and ongoing catalog management.

Royaltē believes artists shouldn't have to become lawyers, accountants, or technology experts to understand these systems. They should simply understand how their music works. Throughout this guide you'll see several Royaltē educational concepts introduced, including Digital Identity™, Catalog Health™, Rights Readiness™, Identity Confidence™, Metadata Drift™, Change Detection™, Career Capital™, and Global Music Footprint™. These concepts are designed to simplify a complicated industry into language artists can actually use.

How to Use This Guide

This isn't a guide you'll read once — it's a reference you'll come back to throughout your career. Each chapter explores one part of the modern music rights ecosystem and links to more detailed articles for readers who want to dive deeper into specific topics. Whether you're trying to understand mechanical royalties, improve your metadata, protect your catalog, or prepare for the future of AI, every chapter builds on the one before it.

By the end, you'll understand not just how artists get paid — but how the entire music rights ecosystem works together to support your career.

Chapter 2 — The Two Copyrights Every Song Creates

Chapter 2 of 12

"Every original song creates two separate pieces of intellectual property. Understanding this single concept is the foundation of the modern music industry."

If you remember only one lesson from this entire guide, make it this one: every original song creates two separate copyrights. Not one. Two.

Surprisingly, many independent artists don't realize this until years into their careers. It's one of the biggest reasons artists become confused about publishing, royalties, ownership, record deals, and music rights. The good news is that once you understand the difference between these two copyrights, the rest of the music business begins to make much more sense.

Why This Matters

Imagine you've just finished writing an incredible song. You spend weeks perfecting the lyrics. You refine the melody. You rehearse the arrangement. Finally, you head into the studio and record it.

When that song is finished, you've actually created two completely different creative works. The first is the song itself. The second is the recorded performance of that song. They work together, but legally — and commercially — they're treated as separate assets.

Copyright #1 — The Musical Composition. The composition is the song itself: the creative idea, including the melody, the lyrics, the chord progression, and the musical structure. If another artist performs your song using completely different instruments, a different singer, and a different producer, the composition is still the same, because the song itself hasn't changed. Think of the composition as the blueprint for a house — it has value before a single brick is laid.

Copyright #2 — The Sound Recording. The second copyright protects the sound recording — the actual recorded performance, the version listeners hear on Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube Music, Amazon Music, TIDAL, and Deezer. Every decision made in the studio contributes to the recording: the vocal performance, the guitar tones, the drum sound, the mix, the mastering. This recording becomes its own valuable piece of intellectual property. Think of it as the finished house built from the blueprint.

One song. Two assets. Neither can replace the other. Together, they create the complete commercial product.

Why This Confuses So Many Artists

Imagine Adele records one of your songs. Her recording sounds nothing like yours — different voice, different musicians, different production. Yet she's still performing your composition. The recording belongs to Adele (or the recording owner). The composition belongs to you (or the composition owner). That's why these copyrights exist separately.

The Two Copyrights Generate Different Opportunities

This distinction becomes incredibly important because each copyright participates in different parts of the music business. Your composition may become connected with music publishing, songwriting royalties, mechanical royalties, and performance royalties. Your sound recording may become connected with distribution, streaming, neighbouring rights, digital performance royalties, and recording ownership.

This is why artists often discover that one song can generate multiple types of royalties through different systems. Understanding the two copyrights is the first step toward understanding how those systems work together.

Ownership Isn't Always Simple

Many independent artists both write and record their own music. In that case, they may initially own both copyrights. But ownership can become more complex over time — a co-writer may own part of the composition, a producer may negotiate ownership in the recording, a record label may acquire rights in the master recording, and a publishing agreement may affect the administration of the composition. The important lesson: the two copyrights don't always share the same owner — ownership should be confirmed, not assumed.

Why Metadata Matters Here

The music industry doesn't recognize your copyrights simply because they exist — it recognizes them because they're documented. That documentation includes information such as song title, songwriters, recording owner, artist name, ISRC, publishing information, ownership details, and contributor credits. Without accurate information, digital systems have a much harder time identifying which composition belongs to which recording. That's why metadata is so important: it connects your copyrights to the broader music ecosystem.

Royaltē's View

Royaltē believes every artist should understand these two copyrights before releasing music. Everything else in the music business — publishing, mechanical royalties, neighbouring rights, record deals, streaming, copyright, ownership, artificial intelligence, metadata — builds from this foundation. Because before your music can generate opportunities, the industry first needs to understand what it is.

Key Takeaways

  • Every original song creates two copyrights.
  • The composition protects the song.
  • The sound recording protects the recorded performance.
  • Understanding this distinction makes every other topic in this guide easier to understand.

Chapter 3 — Every Type of Music Royalty Explained

Chapter 3 of 12

"One song can generate multiple royalties — but only if you understand how the music rights ecosystem works."

One of the biggest misconceptions in the music industry is believing there's only one type of royalty. There isn't. A single song can become connected to multiple royalty streams, each serving a different purpose within the music business. That's why artists are often confused when they hear terms like mechanical royalties, performance royalties, neighbouring rights, streaming royalties, publishing royalties, and synchronization fees. At first it sounds overwhelming — but once you understand why each royalty exists, the entire system becomes much easier to follow.

Think of a Song Like a Business

Imagine opening a coffee shop. Money doesn't come from one customer — revenue comes from coffee sales, food, merchandise, catering, and gift cards. Each source contributes to the overall business. Music works much the same way: a single song can generate value through many different uses, and each use may create a different type of royalty.

The Royaltē Music Rights Ecosystem

Rather than thinking about royalties as separate systems, Royaltē encourages artists to think about them as one connected ecosystem:

Your Song → Two Copyrights Created (Composition · Recording) → Multiple Royalty Opportunities (Publishing · Performance · Mechanical · Neighbouring Rights · Streaming · Licensing · International Collections)

Every royalty begins with the same thing: a song.

A Quick-Reference Overview

Performance royalties Composition Public performance or communication Songwriters, publishers, PROs
Mechanical royalties Composition Eligible reproduction or digital use Songwriters, publishers, administrators

This table is educational, not exhaustive. Eligibility, participants, and typical uses vary by territory, so treat each row as a general pattern rather than a universal rule.

Performance Royalties

Performance royalties are generated when a musical composition is publicly performed or communicated to the public under applicable rights systems — examples include radio, television, live performances, streaming services, restaurants, retail stores, and public venues. These royalties generally relate to the composition rather than the recording, which is one reason songwriters often join Performing Rights Organizations (PROs).

Mechanical Royalties

Mechanical royalties relate to certain reproductions and uses of the musical composition. Historically, they were associated with physical music reproduction; today they remain an important part of the digital music ecosystem. Mechanical royalties recognize the value of the underlying song, not the recording — if you're a songwriter, understanding mechanical royalties is essential.

Neighbouring Rights

Neighbouring rights recognize the value of the recorded performance. Unlike publishing, which focuses on the composition, neighbouring rights focus on the recording itself, and may involve featured performers, recording owners, and record labels. Depending on the country and applicable laws, neighbouring rights may be generated when commercially released recordings are publicly used.

Streaming Royalties

Streaming has changed how music reaches listeners. When someone presses Play, multiple rights may become involved behind the scenes — streaming itself isn't one single royalty, but may involve several different royalty streams working together depending on the country, applicable rights, licensing arrangements, rights organizations, and platform agreements. This is why streaming often appears more complicated than artists expect.

Synchronization (Sync)

Synchronization happens when music is paired with visual media — movies, television, commercials, video games, online advertisements, and streaming productions. Because synchronization uses both the composition and the recording, permissions may be required from multiple rights holders depending on the project. For many artists, sync licensing represents an exciting opportunity to expand both their audience and revenue potential.

Digital Performance Royalties

Some countries recognize additional royalty types for certain digital performances of sound recordings. One well-known example in the United States involves SoundExchange, which helps administer certain digital performance royalties under U.S. law for eligible performers and rights owners. Understanding which organizations administer which rights is an important part of building a complete music business.

International Royalties

Music doesn't stop at national borders — your song may be streamed in Germany, played on radio in Australia, performed publicly in Canada, or licensed in Japan. Each country operates within its own legal and administrative framework. International agreements help support the global administration of music rights, allowing artists to benefit from worldwide audiences.

Why One Song Creates Multiple Royalties

Imagine your newest single becomes successful. A listener streams it. A radio station plays it. A television show licenses it. A café includes it in its daily playlist. Another artist records a cover version. Although it's the same song, each use interacts with different parts of the music rights ecosystem — that's why multiple royalty opportunities may exist. The song is working in different ways.

The Biggest Misunderstanding

Many artists believe: "My distributor collects every royalty." In reality, distributors, publishers, Performing Rights Organizations, neighbouring rights organizations, and other industry participants all play different roles within the broader ecosystem. No single organization manages every aspect of every music right. Understanding that distinction is one of the biggest milestones in becoming an informed independent artist.

Royalties Begin With Preparation

Royalties don't begin when money arrives — they begin long before release day. Every royalty system depends on accurate information, including ownership, metadata, registrations, publishing information, recording information, and Digital Identity™. The stronger your foundation, the better prepared your catalog becomes to participate in today's music industry.

Royaltē calls this Rights Readiness™ — a measure of how prepared your music is to participate across the global music rights ecosystem. It isn't about chasing royalties. It's about ensuring your music has the information needed to support every opportunity available to it.

Royalties Are the Result — Not the Beginning

Royalties don't exist in isolation. They are the outcome of many systems working together: good metadata, clear ownership, complete registrations, publishing, catalog management, and digital identity. Each one strengthens the foundation supporting your music, and the better that foundation becomes, the better prepared your catalog is for long-term success.

Chapter 4 — Music Publishing: The Most Misunderstood Part of the Music Business

Chapter 4 of 12

"Music publishing isn't about publishing music. It's about protecting, managing, and administering the value of the songs you write."

If there is one topic that confuses more independent artists than any other, it's music publishing. Ask ten musicians what a music publisher does and you'll probably receive ten different answers. Some believe publishing means uploading music to Spotify. Others think it's the same thing as distribution. Some assume only major-label artists need publishers. None of those are entirely correct. The truth is that music publishing has very little to do with releasing music — it has everything to do with the song itself.

What Is Music Publishing?

Music publishing revolves around one thing: the musical composition. Not the recording, not the mix, not the master — the composition, meaning the melody, lyrics, and musical arrangement. Whenever you write an original song, you've created a composition, and that composition becomes intellectual property. Music publishing exists to help manage that intellectual property throughout its life.

As we learned in Chapter 2, every original song creates two copyrights: the composition and the sound recording. Publishing focuses on the composition; record labels generally focus on the sound recording. This simple distinction explains why publishing and record deals are completely different parts of the music business.

Think of Publishing Like Property Management

Imagine owning an apartment building. Owning the building is one thing; managing it is another — someone needs to keep records, track tenants, collect payments, maintain documentation, and handle administration. Music publishing works similarly: the songwriter owns the creative work, and publishing helps manage the business surrounding that work. This doesn't mean every songwriter needs a publisher — it means every songwriter should understand what publishing actually does.

Publishing Is Not Distribution

This is one of the biggest misconceptions in the music industry. Distribution answers the question, "How does my recording reach listeners?" Publishing answers a completely different question: "How is my composition represented within the music rights ecosystem?" They work together, but they solve completely different problems.

What Does a Music Publisher Actually Do?

While services vary, publishing companies and publishing administrators may assist with registering compositions, maintaining songwriter information, managing ownership records, administering publishing rights, supporting royalty administration, licensing opportunities, and catalog management. Think of publishing as the business infrastructure supporting your songwriting.

Not every artist signs with a traditional publisher. Many independent artists instead work with publishing administrators, who generally focus on helping administer the business side of your compositions while allowing you to retain ownership of your songs. The exact services vary between companies, but publishing administration has become an increasingly popular option for independent artists who want professional support while maintaining greater control over their catalogs.

Songwriters Own More Than Songs

Every composition you write becomes part of your catalog of songs, and over time that collection becomes something much larger. Some songs may become commercial successes; others may never be released. All contribute to the value of your creative legacy.

Publishing Supports Long-Term Careers

Many artists think only about their newest release. Professional songwriters think about decades. Songs written today may continue creating opportunities years — or even decades — from now, through singles, albums, co-writes, film and television placements, covers by other artists, and international opportunities. Publishing helps support that long-term perspective.

Common Publishing Myths

  • "My distributor handles my publishing." Not necessarily — distribution and publishing serve different purposes.
  • "I'm not famous enough to worry about publishing." Publishing isn't about fame. It's about songwriting. If you write original music, publishing matters.
  • "I'll think about publishing later." Many artists do. The strongest music businesses are often built by understanding these systems early rather than waiting until success arrives.

Publishing Readiness™

Royaltē refers to this as Publishing Readiness™ — a measure of how prepared your compositions are to participate within today's global music publishing ecosystem, including song ownership, songwriter information, registrations, administration, metadata, and rights documentation. Publishing Readiness™ isn't about predicting success. It's about preparing your songs for opportunity.

Your Songs Are Intellectual Property

One of the biggest mindset shifts an artist can make is realizing that songs aren't just creative works — they're intellectual property, with the potential to be licensed, performed, covered, streamed, and discovered. Understanding publishing helps artists think beyond today's release and toward tomorrow's opportunities.

How Royaltē Helps

Royaltē doesn't replace publishers or publishing administrators. Royaltē helps artists better understand the information that supports their songwriting — by reviewing your Digital Identity™, Publishing Readiness™, Rights Readiness™, metadata, registrations, and ownership information, Royaltē helps artists gain greater visibility into the business behind their compositions. Because writing a great song is only the beginning; understanding how that song lives within the music industry is what helps transform creativity into a lasting career.

Chapter 5 — Metadata: The Digital Foundation of Every Song

Chapter 5 of 12

"Your audience hears your music. The music industry reads your metadata."

Imagine releasing the greatest song you've ever written. The performance is flawless. The production is world-class. The mix is exceptional. Thousands of people begin listening. Now imagine one small piece of information is wrong: your artist name is inconsistent, a songwriter credit is missing, an identifier wasn't assigned correctly. The music still sounds exactly the same — but suddenly, the systems responsible for organizing, identifying, and connecting your music begin encountering problems. That's the power of metadata. It doesn't change how your music sounds. It changes how the music industry understands it.

What Is Metadata?

Metadata is simply information about your music. It describes your recordings and compositions so digital systems can identify, organize, and connect them — telling platforms who created the music, what the recording is, which composition it belongs to, who owns what, when it was released, and how it connects to the rest of your catalog. Without metadata, digital music platforms would struggle to distinguish one recording from another.

Think of metadata like a passport. Imagine arriving at an international airport without one — you know who you are, your family knows who you are, but border officials have no reliable way to verify your identity. Metadata works much the same way: digital systems require standardized information before they can confidently recognize, organize, and connect your work across the global music ecosystem.

What Information Does Metadata Include?

While requirements vary between platforms, common information includes:

  • Artist information — artist name, featured artists, collaborators
  • Song information — song title, album title, track number, release date
  • Songwriting information — songwriters, composers, lyricists
  • Recording information — recording owner, record label, producer, mix engineer, mastering engineer
  • Identifiers — ISRC, UPC, catalog numbers
  • Rights information — publishing information, ownership details, contributor credits

Metadata Is the Language of the Music Industry

Musicians communicate through sound. Digital platforms communicate through data. Metadata is the common language that connects those two worlds. When metadata is complete and consistent, platforms have a much better understanding of your music; when it's incomplete or inconsistent, confusion can occur. This is why accuracy matters so much.

Twenty years ago, metadata was largely an administrative necessity. Today it has become a strategic asset — modern music platforms use it to organize catalogs, recommend music, connect releases, display credits, support search, and improve discovery. As artificial intelligence becomes more integrated into music platforms, structured and accurate information becomes even more valuable, since AI doesn't "listen" to music the way humans do — it analyzes enormous amounts of structured information. While metadata alone doesn't guarantee discovery, it provides important context that supports modern digital music ecosystems.

The Hidden Cost of Bad Metadata

Metadata problems are rarely dramatic — they're usually small: one missing credit, one inconsistent spelling, one incorrect identifier, one duplicated release. Individually, these issues may seem insignificant; over time, they can create friction across your catalog. Royaltē refers to this gradual accumulation of inconsistencies as Metadata Drift™ — it doesn't happen overnight, it happens one small inconsistency at a time.

Many artists think metadata is something completed on release day. In reality, it evolves — new collaborators join projects, credits are updated, ownership changes, publishing relationships change, and catalogs grow. The stronger your catalog becomes, the more important ongoing metadata management becomes.

Metadata Confidence™

Metadata doesn't exist in isolation — it forms the foundation of what Royaltē calls your Digital Identity™, which Chapter 6 explores in full.

Royaltē introduces one metadata-specific concept here: Metadata Confidence™ — the degree of trust that can be placed in the consistency, completeness, and quality of the information describing your catalog. High Metadata Confidence™ doesn't guarantee success; it provides confidence that your catalog is being represented clearly and consistently across the digital ecosystem.

Artists often invest thousands of dollars into recording, mixing, mastering, photography, and marketing, yet many spend very little time reviewing the information describing those same releases. Metadata deserves the same level of attention as every other part of your music business — because once your music enters the world, metadata becomes the language every digital platform uses to understand it.

How Royaltē Helps

Royaltē doesn't replace distributors or metadata providers. Royaltē helps artists better understand the information supporting their music — by reviewing your Digital Identity™, Metadata Confidence™, Catalog Health™, and Global Music Footprint™, Royaltē helps artists identify opportunities to strengthen the foundation supporting their catalog. Because metadata isn't paperwork. It's infrastructure.

Chapter 6 — Your Digital Identity™: The Foundation of Your Music Career

Chapter 6 of 12

"Before the music industry can understand your songs, it first has to understand you."

Imagine searching for your favorite artist online. Within seconds, you can usually find their official artist profile, albums, singles, collaborators, songwriting credits, social media accounts, and biography. It all feels seamless — but behind that experience is an enormous amount of interconnected information. Every streaming platform, music database, rights organization, search engine, and AI system is trying to answer the same fundamental question: "Who is this artist?" That question is far more complicated than it appears, and the answer forms the foundation of what Royaltē calls your Digital Identity™.

What Is a Digital Identity™?

Your Digital Identity™ is the complete picture of your professional presence across the global music ecosystem — much more than your artist name. It includes your artist name, official artist profiles, releases, songwriting credits, collaborations, identifiers, metadata, publishing information, rights information, and public catalog. Every one of these elements contributes to how the industry understands you.

Earlier we compared metadata to your music's passport. Your Digital Identity™ is more like your entire passport file — travel history, citizenship records, visas, and more, all agreeing with one another to create confidence about who you are. No single piece of information defines an artist; confidence comes from many pieces of information agreeing with one another.

Your Name Isn't Enough

One of the biggest misconceptions artists have is believing their artist name uniquely identifies them. It doesn't — thousands of musicians around the world share similar or identical names. Without additional identifying information, confusion can happen. Your Digital Identity™ helps reduce that uncertainty, and it's built through consistency: presenting the same accurate information — consistent artist name, accurate release information, correct songwriting credits, complete metadata, connected artist profiles, up-to-date ownership details — across every platform and service that represents your music.

Your Digital Identity™ isn't created overnight. It grows over time — every release becomes another chapter in your professional story, every collaboration expands your network, and every songwriting credit strengthens your portfolio. Identity is cumulative.

Identity Confidence™ and Identity Coverage™

Royaltē refers to the strength of this ecosystem as Identity Confidence™ — how clearly and consistently your music business is represented across the digital ecosystem. A high level of Identity Confidence™ means the available information paints a clear, consistent picture of your professional identity; lower confidence may indicate areas where information could be incomplete, inconsistent, or disconnected. It's not about judging your career — it's about understanding the quality of the information surrounding it.

Most identity issues don't happen because of one major mistake. They happen because of many small inconsistencies — a changed name spelling, a differently-credited featured artist, outdated information on one platform. Individually minor, these differences can collectively make it harder for digital systems to confidently understand how everything fits together.

Royaltē also introduces Identity Coverage™ — how completely your professional music identity is represented across the ecosystem. It looks beyond simply asking whether you have an artist profile, and instead asks whether your profiles are connected, your information is consistent, and your identity is complete. Identity isn't measured by quantity. It's measured by completeness.

Digital Identity™ and Artificial Intelligence

As AI becomes increasingly integrated into the music industry, identity becomes even more important. AI systems don't simply analyze songs — they analyze relationships between artists, songs, albums, collaborators, genres, and credits. The clearer those relationships become, the more context AI has available. That's why building a strong Digital Identity™ isn't just about today's music platforms — it's about preparing for tomorrow's technology.

A Digital Identity™ isn't static. It changes throughout your career as you release new music, collaborate, sign agreements, and expand internationally — which makes ongoing review just as important as the initial setup.

Chapter 7 — Music Distribution: Getting Your Music Into the World

Chapter 7 of 12

"A music distributor delivers your recording to listeners. It doesn't manage every part of your music business."

For many independent artists, releasing music has never been easier. With just a few clicks, you can upload a song through a digital distributor and have it available on hundreds of streaming platforms around the world. Artists no longer need a major record label to reach a global audience. But this accessibility has also created one of the biggest misconceptions in modern music: that once music is uploaded through a distributor, everything else is taken care of. Understanding what distributors do — and just as importantly, what they don't do — is critical to building a successful, sustainable music business.

What Is Music Distribution?

At its core, music distribution is the process of delivering your recordings to digital music services such as Apple Music, Spotify, Amazon Music, YouTube Music, Deezer, and TIDAL. Without distributors, getting music onto these platforms would be significantly more difficult for independent artists.

Think of a distributor like a shipping company. Your job is creating the product; the shipping company doesn't design it, own it, or advertise it — it simply delivers it to retailers so customers can buy it. Distributors commonly help artists by delivering music to streaming platforms, managing release scheduling, processing supplied metadata, managing takedowns or updates, and providing reporting. Many also offer additional optional services depending on the company and subscription level.

What Distributors Don't Do

This is where many artists become confused. Uploading your music through a distributor doesn't automatically mean every aspect of your music career has been addressed — distribution and rights administration are different responsibilities. Depending on your situation, you may still need to consider songwriting administration, publishing, performance rights, neighbouring rights, metadata management, catalog maintenance, copyright strategy, and international rights administration. Your distributor plays an important role, but it isn't the entire music business.

The Independent Artist Music Rights Checklist

A quick reference for reviewing your music business. This checklist is a starting point for organizing your information — it doesn't guarantee payment, registration acceptance, or legal compliance.

  • Confirm artist-name consistency across every platform and profile.
  • Document writers and ownership splits for every composition.
  • Maintain accurate contributor credits (producers, mixers, featured artists).
  • Preserve master and composition records, including dated project files.
  • Review publishing and PRO information for completeness.
  • Understand the roles of your distributor and any publishing administrator.
  • Review the mechanical and neighbouring-rights systems relevant to your territory.
  • Confirm recording identifiers (ISRC, UPC) are correctly assigned and not duplicated.
  • Review catalog metadata at least once a year.
  • Monitor international availability and territory-specific representation.
  • Maintain secure copies of agreements, registrations, and contracts.
  • Review your business whenever collaborators, ownership, or service providers change.
  • Seek qualified professional advice when necessary.

Glossary

Composition
The musical and lyrical work itself: melody, lyrics, and arrangement. One of the two copyrights every original song creates.
Sound recording (master)
The recorded performance of a composition. The second of the two copyrights every original song creates.
Copyright
Legal protection that generally applies to original creative works, including compositions and sound recordings, though specific rules and durations vary by jurisdiction.
Music publishing
The business of managing, administering, and protecting the value of a musical composition.
Publishing administrator
A company that helps administer the business side of a songwriter's compositions, typically without acquiring ownership.
Performing Rights Organization (PRO)
An organization that helps collect performance royalties on behalf of songwriters and publishers.
Mechanical royalty
A royalty relating to certain reproductions and uses of a musical composition.
Performance royalty
A royalty generated when a composition is publicly performed or communicated to the public.
Neighbouring rights
Rights recognizing the value of a recorded performance, distinct from the composition.
Digital performance royalty
A royalty type recognized by some countries for certain digital performances of sound recordings (in the U.S., administered in part by SoundExchange).
Distributor
A company that delivers recordings to digital music services on an artist's behalf.
ISRC (International Standard Recording Code)
A unique identifier assigned to a specific sound recording.
UPC (Universal Product Code)
A unique identifier assigned to a release (single, EP, or album).
Metadata
Structured information describing a recording or composition, used by digital systems to identify, organize, and connect music.
Digital Identity™
Royaltē's term for the complete picture of an artist's professional presence across the global music ecosystem.
Catalog
An artist's complete collection of releases and recordings.
Sync licensing
The licensing of music for use alongside visual media, such as film, television, or advertising.
Rights holder
Any individual or entity holding ownership or administrative rights in a composition or sound recording.
Collection society
An organization that collects and distributes certain royalties on behalf of rights holders, subject to its own rules and territory.
Territory
The country or region within which a specific right, registration, or agreement applies.
Global Music Footprint™
Royaltē's term for how an artist's catalog is represented across the international music ecosystem.
