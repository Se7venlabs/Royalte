Every day, thousands of independent artists upload new music to streaming platforms through digital distributors. Within hours or days, their songs appear on Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube Music, Amazon Music, and dozens of other services around the world.

For many artists, that feels like the finish line.

In reality, it's only the beginning.

One of the biggest misconceptions in the music industry is believing that your music distributor collects every royalty your songs generate. While distributors play a critical role in getting your music heard, they are only one part of a much larger music rights ecosystem.

Understanding the difference could mean the difference between collecting the royalties you've earned—or leaving money behind.

What Does a Music Distributor Actually Do?

A digital music distributor helps deliver your recordings to streaming services and online music stores.

Most distributors are responsible for:

  • Delivering your music to digital platforms.
  • Managing your releases.
  • Collecting eligible recording royalties from participating services.
  • Reporting streams and downloads.
  • Paying you according to their agreement.

Without distributors, independent artists would have a much harder time reaching global audiences.

But distributors are not designed to manage every aspect of music rights administration.

One Song Can Generate Multiple Royalties

Here's where many artists become confused.

A single song isn't tied to just one royalty payment.

Every time your music is streamed, downloaded, broadcast, publicly performed, or licensed, different types of royalties may be generated depending on how it's used.

These may include:

  • Recording royalties
  • Performance royalties
  • Mechanical royalties
  • Publishing royalties
  • Neighbouring rights
  • International royalties

Your distributor generally collects only the royalties they are authorized to administer.

Other royalties are collected through completely different organizations and rights systems.

Distributor vs. Publisher: Understanding the Difference

One of the most common misunderstandings in the music industry is confusing a music distributor with a music publisher.

Although both play important roles, they serve very different purposes.

A music distributor focuses on delivering your sound recordings to digital platforms and collecting eligible recording income from those services.

A music publisher or publishing administrator focuses on the underlying musical composition—the lyrics and melody you've written—and helps administer publishing-related royalties.

In simple terms:

  • Your distributor manages your recordings.
  • Your publisher manages your songwriting rights.

They complement each other, but they are not the same thing.

Why Artists Miss Royalties

Most missing royalties don't happen because artists aren't talented.

They happen because the music rights system is incredibly complex.

Common reasons include:

  • Missing registrations
  • Incomplete songwriter information
  • Incorrect ownership splits
  • Metadata errors
  • Missing publishing administration
  • Unclaimed neighbouring rights
  • International registrations that were never completed

Your music may still be available everywhere online, but that doesn't necessarily mean every royalty is finding its way back to you.

The Registration Gap™

Royaltē refers to this as the Registration Gap™.

It's the difference between the royalties your catalog has the potential to generate and the royalties you're actually positioned to collect.

Imagine filling a bucket with water that has several small holes in the bottom.

No single leak may seem significant, but over time they add up.

The same is true for your music catalog.

Every missing registration, incorrect metadata field, or incomplete rights record can create another opportunity for revenue to be delayed, misdirected, or remain unclaimed.

Distribution Is Only One Step

Uploading your music is an important milestone—but it's only one part of building a professional music career.

Successful artists also pay attention to:

  • Accurate metadata
  • Songwriter registrations
  • Publishing administration
  • Performance rights
  • Neighbouring rights
  • International royalty collection
  • Catalog management

The more complete your registrations become, the stronger your royalty foundation becomes.

Your Catalog Continues Working Long After Release Day

Many artists focus all of their energy on release week.

But your catalog doesn't stop working once launch day is over.

Songs can continue generating value through:

  • Playlist placements
  • Radio airplay
  • Public performances
  • International streaming
  • Licensing opportunities
  • New listeners discovering older releases

A well-managed catalog becomes an asset that can continue generating revenue for years.

That's why regularly reviewing your registrations and rights information is just as important as creating new music.

Knowledge Is One of Your Greatest Assets

The music business isn't only built on creativity.

It's also built on information.

Understanding how royalties flow, where they're collected, and which organizations are responsible for different rights allows artists to make smarter decisions about their careers.

The goal isn't to become an expert in every legal detail.

The goal is to understand enough to recognize opportunities, ask better questions, and ensure your catalog is positioned to earn everything it's entitled to earn.
