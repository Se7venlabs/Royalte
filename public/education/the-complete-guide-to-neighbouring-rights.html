If you've spent time learning about music royalties, you've probably heard terms like:

  • Publishing royalties
  • Mechanical royalties
  • Performance royalties

But there's another type of royalty that many artists don't discover until years into their careers.

It's called Neighbouring Rights.

For some artists, neighbouring rights represent a valuable source of income they didn't even know existed.

The challenge is that they're also one of the least understood parts of the global music industry.

The good news?

Once you understand what neighbouring rights are, who they're for, and how they're collected, the entire system becomes much easier to understand.

What Are Neighbouring Rights?

Neighbouring Rights are royalties associated with the public use of sound recordings.

Unlike publishing royalties, which relate to the underlying composition (the melody and lyrics), neighbouring rights relate to the recorded performance itself.

They recognize the contribution of:

  • Featured performers
  • Session musicians (where applicable)
  • Record labels
  • Owners of the sound recording

In many countries, when a commercially released recording is publicly performed or broadcast, neighbouring rights royalties may be generated for eligible rights holders.

Why Are They Called "Neighbouring" Rights?

The name often confuses people.

They're called Neighbouring Rights because they exist alongside traditional copyright—not instead of it.

Think of it this way.

Every commercial song generally involves two important creative assets:

The Composition

The melody and lyrics written by the songwriter.

The Sound Recording

The recorded performance listeners hear.

Publishing protects the composition.

Neighbouring Rights relate to the recording.

They are neighbours in the music rights ecosystem.

Composition vs. Recording

Royaltē often explains this using a simple example.

Imagine a movie.

The screenplay is one creative work.

The finished movie is another.

Music works the same way.

The composition is like the screenplay.

The recording is like the finished movie.

Neighbouring Rights recognize the value of that finished performance.

When Can Neighbouring Rights Be Generated?

Neighbouring rights may arise when commercially released recordings are publicly used.

Examples include:

  • Radio broadcasts
  • Television broadcasts
  • Certain public performances
  • Commercial venues
  • International broadcasts

The exact circumstances depend on the laws, agreements, and rights systems in each country.

Not every country operates under the same rules, which is one reason neighbouring rights can seem complicated.

Why Many Artists Miss This Revenue

One of the biggest reasons artists overlook neighbouring rights is simple:

No one tells them they exist.

Many artists assume their distributor collects every royalty.

Others believe their Performing Rights Organization (PRO) collects everything.

Neither assumption is always correct.

Neighbouring rights are often administered through specialized organizations, depending on where your music is used.

Understanding who collects what is an important part of building a complete royalty strategy.

Meet the Organizations

Different countries have different organizations responsible for administering neighbouring rights.

Here are a few of the best-known examples.

SoundExchange (United States)

For many artists, SoundExchange is the first neighbouring rights organization they encounter.

SoundExchange helps administer certain digital performance royalties in the United States for eligible featured artists, rights owners, and other qualifying performers under U.S. law.

For many independent artists, registering with SoundExchange is an important part of building a complete royalty foundation.

PPL (United Kingdom)

In the United Kingdom, PPL administers neighbouring rights for eligible performers and recording rightsholders.

When commercially released recordings are publicly performed or broadcast under applicable rules, PPL helps collect and distribute royalties to eligible members.

Re:Sound (Canada)

In Canada, Re:Sound plays a similar role.

Re:Sound helps license the public use of recorded music and administers neighbouring rights royalties for eligible performers and recording owners under Canadian law.

For Canadian artists, understanding Re:Sound is an important part of understanding the country's music rights ecosystem.

International Collections

Music travels farther than ever before.

A song released in Toronto might be played in London.

A recording created in Nashville might be broadcast in Australia.

An independent artist from Brazil might find listeners in Germany.

Neighbouring rights don't stop at national borders.

International agreements and reciprocal relationships between organizations help support the administration of royalties across many countries.

Because music is global, rights administration increasingly needs to be global as well.

Your Recording Has Value Beyond Streaming

Many artists think only about Spotify, Apple Music, and YouTube Music.

But recordings may continue generating value through many different forms of public use.

Understanding neighbouring rights helps artists recognize that a recording's value extends beyond streaming platforms alone.

Your music may continue creating opportunities long after release day.
